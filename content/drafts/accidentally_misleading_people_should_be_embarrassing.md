Title: Accidentally Misleading People Should Be Embarrassing
Date: 2027-01-01
Status: draft
Category: philosophy
Tags: honesty

Sometimes we say things that we think are true—perhaps even things that _are_ true—which end up causing others to conclude something false. It's tempting to relate to such a situation primarily in terms of maintaining the innocence of one's soul—to say, fine, my readers became more wrong, but I'm not to be [judged](https://zackmdavis.net/blog/2025/Oct/the-relationship-between-social-punishment-and-shared-maps/) for it because I believed what I was saying; I wasn't _being misleading_ just because the people who believed me were thereby led into falsehood.

The temptation reflects a confusion as to the nature of the judgment. Mostly, people don't care about judging the innocence of your soul; they care about whether they'll be led into falsehood the next time they take the risk of believing what you say—if there is a next time. If you want there to be a next time and you're going to have social emotions about it, the most productive emotion is _embarrassment_ rather than defensiveness. Visibly embarrassed people can be counted on to work hard to make sure that people won't regret believing them next time. Defensive people won't, because, well, why would they? They didn't do anything wrong.

Once, I suspected an ideological rival of misrepresenting a citation: the text of their claim linked to a webpage featuring an embedded PDF of an essay, but when I used my web browser's "Ctrl-F" search functionality with appropriate search terms to see what in the paper justified the claim, I came up empty. When considering whether to escalate to complaining more loudly about my rival over this (after the rival didn't respond to a blog comment and DM), I mentioned the matter to a third party—who pointed out that the essay _did_ contain my search term! On further testing, it appeared that my web browser's Ctrl-F behavior depended on which page element had focus: if the user had last clicked inside the embedded PDF viewer, the search returned results inside the PDF, but not if the user had last clicked elsewhere on the page.

I was _so embarrassed_. 

To be clear, I knew I was innocent in my soul: the browser UX bug wasn't my fault, and if the browser hadn't been buggy, then my initial search would have succeeded and I never would have complained. I [certainly didn't _lie_](https://zackmdavis.net/blog/2019/Dec/firming-up-not-lying-around-its-edge-cases-is-less-broadly-useful-than-one-might-initially-think/). (I didn't say that I read the essay and verified that it didn't support the claim, which was false; I said the Ctrl-F search came up empty, which was true.)

But the innocence of my soul didn't matter. I knew that raising a suspicion that turned out to be false _looked bad for me_, even if there was ultimately a perfectly reasonable explanation for my actions. If I didn't want to look bad, I couldn't count on everyone hanging around to listen to my perfectly reasonable explanation for why it was all the Firefox developers' fault. I would need to make some sort of generalizable change to my policy—perhaps, reading the entire PDF myself next time before raising the suspicion that an ideological rival was misrepresenting it.

(Recounting this seven years later, I went back and downloaded the PDF. Excluding the title page and the endnotes, it was six pages.)

Another time, a journalist wrote an article paraphrasing a source as saying that journalists "discussing both critics and supporters" of some controversial public figure "could be unfair" and should instead "somehow prove statistically which side was right." I was familiar with the source's work, and the paraphrase didn't sound like something she would say, so I asked her about it. She said she had told the journalist that she "wasn't a huge fan of 'both sides' journalism, where you just find someone with one opinion and someone with the other opinion" rather than trying to figure out the truth and had given suggestions for data sources that might bear on claims about the public figure.
